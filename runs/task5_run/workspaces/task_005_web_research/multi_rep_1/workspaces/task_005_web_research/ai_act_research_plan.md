Most recent milestone (conservative statement):
- By mid-2024 the EU AI Act text had been finalized in political agreement and was moving toward formal adoption and publication; Member States and the Commission were preparing implementation steps including the creation of the European AI Office and an EU database for certain AI systems.
- Specific enforcement (application) dates and phased obligations (e.g., when high-risk conformity assessments, registration obligations, and general-purpose/foundation-model obligations begin to apply) were expected to follow the Act's entry into force and may include transitional periods.

Which obligations typically apply next for product teams:
- Determine whether your system is in a prohibited category, high-risk category, or subject to transparency obligations (e.g., AI that interacts with humans, emotion recognition, biometric categorization).
- If high-risk: prepare a documented risk-management system, technical documentation, data governance and data quality measures, human oversight arrangements, conformity assessment (internal or third-party), CE-marking / declaration of conformity, post-market monitoring plan, and registration in the EU database (when required).
- For providers of general-purpose/foundation models (if covered by the final text): prepare safety documentation, incident reporting processes, and transparency measures; watch for separate obligations specifically targeted at foundation models.
- For generative models: prepare transparency/disclosure measures, watermarking and provenance tooling where applicable, and misuse risk assessments.

Uncertainties and timing ambiguity product teams should track:
- Exact entry-into-force date and the date specific obligations begin to apply (there are often delays/transitional periods after publication in the Official Journal).
- Final definitions and scope (e.g., the legal tests for what counts as a "high-risk AI system", "provider", "operator", and whether specific product lines or cloud services are in scope).
- Timing and operational readiness of the European AI Office and national competent authorities (these bodies' readiness affects enforcement timing and practical guidance availability).
- Availability of harmonized standards and conformity-assessment procedures (standardization timelines affect how to demonstrate compliance).
- Interaction with GDPR, NIS2, and national rules; potential duplicative or differing obligations.
- How regulators will prioritize enforcement (some provisions may attract immediate scrutiny, others may be staged).
