Short summary (as of 15 March 2026)

1) Most recent enforcement milestone
- The most recent binding (legal) milestones are the staged application dates in the AI Act itself: Chapters I–II (including the prohibition list) applied from 2 February 2025; Chapter III §4, Chapter V (the rules for general‑purpose AI models), governance and penalties became applicable from 2 August 2025. The main remaining “full application” date for the Act is 2 August 2026 (most substantive high‑risk obligations). (Primary source: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Article 113). ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))

- Important recent policy developments (that affect rollout/timing but are not law): the European Commission published a “Digital Omnibus” legislative proposal on 19 November 2025 (to amend parts of the AI Act among other digital rules), and the EDPB & EDPS issued a joint opinion on that proposal in January–February 2026. These are active policy milestones that could change application details or timing, but they are proposals/opinions (not yet binding). ([aiactblog.nl](https://www.aiactblog.nl/en/posts/digital-omnibus-ai-act-simplification-or-erosion?utm_source=openai))

2) Which obligations apply next (i.e., what product teams should expect to become binding soon)
- Next scheduled / looming obligations (what to prepare for now):
  - The “high‑risk AI systems” obligations (Annex III systems / Chapter III Section 2 requirements) are the main set of obligations that become generally applicable on 2 August 2026. These include (inter alia): risk‑management systems, technical documentation (Annex IV), record‑keeping / automatic logging, post‑market monitoring plans and reporting, human‑oversight measures, conformity assessment procedures / involvement of notified bodies where required, registration in the EU database for certain systems, and incident/serious‑incident reporting. (Primary source: AI Act text — Annex III, Annex IV, Articles on post‑market monitoring, conformity assessment and Article 113 dates). ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
  - Note also: providers of general‑purpose AI models have obligations that have been applicable since 2 August 2025 (technical documentation, training‑data summaries, copyright reservation policy, cooperation with the AI Office). Product teams using or integrating GPAI must already be following those obligations where they apply. (AI Act Chapter V / Article 53). ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
  - A further (later) set of obligations for high‑risk AI that are part of product safety sectoral integration apply 36 months after entry (e.g., 2 August 2027 for some Article 6(1) items) — check Article 113 / transitional rules for product‑embedded AI. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))

3) Key uncertainties a product team should track (practical risks / timing ambiguities)
- Legislative change risk: the Commission’s Digital Omnibus (proposal published 19 Nov 2025) seeks to simplify/adjust implementation details (including mechanisms that could defer some deadlines or change registration/reporting obligations). It is an active legislative file (EP/Council/ trilogue stage) and may alter deadlines or procedural rules — but it is not law yet. Treat any timetable changes as possible but not guaranteed until the Omnibus is formally adopted (or rejected). ([aiactblog.nl](https://www.aiactblog.nl/en/posts/digital-omnibus-ai-act-simplification-or-erosion?utm_source=openai))
- Standards / compliance instruments availability: many compliance duties are framed to work with harmonised technical standards, common specifications and codes of practice. Work on harmonised standards (CEN/CENELEC, ETSI, etc.) has lagged relative to the Act’s staggered dates; if harmonised standards/common specifications are not published in time, the Commission/legislature (or the Omnibus) may postpone or provide alternative compliance mechanisms. This creates operational uncertainty for teams aiming to rely on “presumption of conformity” via harmonised standards. ([taylorwessing.com](https://www.taylorwessing.com/zh-hant/insights-and-events/aiq?utm_source=openai))
- Guidance / classification ambiguity: the Act tasks the Commission and the European Artificial Intelligence Board with guidelines and classification rules (e.g., practical guidance on Article 6 / what counts as high‑risk) by set dates (the Act itself foresees Commission guidelines and delegated acts). In practice those guidelines and templates (e.g., post‑market monitoring template, GPAI Code of Practice adequacy assessments) have been slower to appear or are still being finalised — so there is short‑term ambiguity over how national authorities will classify systems and how auditors/notified bodies will apply tests. Product teams should not assume uniform interpretations across Member States. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
- National variance and enforcement capacity: the EU Act creates an EU governance framework (European AI Office, European Artificial Intelligence Board) plus national competent authorities and notified bodies. Member States are still designating authorities and building capacity; that means enforcement/interpretation may vary by country and initial enforcement intensity will depend on national resources and priorities. The Act’s reports (required after application) will explicitly evaluate national resources. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
- Interaction with data protection / other laws: obligations (e.g., logging, post‑market monitoring, bias testing) can require processing of personal or special‑category data; the EDPB/EDPS joint opinion stresses the need for strong safeguards and clarifies tensions with the GDPR. Expect detailed cross‑regulatory guidance and possible negotiation on what processing is lawful for compliance purposes. ([edpb.europa.eu](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/consistency-findings/opinions_en?utm_source=openai))
- Timing ambiguity & “what to plan for” rule of thumb:
  - Plan on the Act’s current dates as the baseline: in particular, prepare for the Annex III high‑risk obligations to become applicable on 2 August 2026. (EUR‑Lex / Article 113). ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
  - Simultaneously, track the Omnibus procedure and standardisation updates closely (these could delay specific obligations or change the “how” of compliance). Do not pause compliance work waiting for a final decision — instead phase effort and document decisions/assessments in case obligations are later enforced or inspected. (Practical guidance from legal/industry trackers). ([aiactblog.nl](https://www.aiactblog.nl/en/posts/digital-omnibus-ai-act-simplification-or-erosion?utm_source=openai))

Concrete next steps for a product team (practical checklist)
- Map your systems to the AI Act risk tiers now (minimal / limited / high / prohibited / GPAI). Keep the Article 6 / Annex III criteria and the Commission’s forthcoming classifications in view. Document the assessments you make (self‑assessment records are specifically required if you consider an Annex III system not high‑risk). ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
- Start or mature risk‑management and post‑market monitoring processes now (these are core to high‑risk compliance). Draft technical documentation using Annex IV elements so you are not building from scratch if/when conformity assessment starts. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
- Track standards and the GPAI Code of Practice (CoP) process closely; if a harmonised standard or approved CoP is available for your use, reliance on it gives a presumption of conformity. If not, plan to demonstrate equivalent measures and keep records. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
- Monitor the Digital Omnibus legislative file, EDPB/EDPS opinions, and national competent authority notices for changes or guidance that affect your deliverables; log decisions and compliance timelines internally. ([aiactblog.nl](https://www.aiactblog.nl/en/posts/digital-omnibus-ai-act-simplification-or-erosion?utm_source=openai))

Explicit statement of uncertainty / timing ambiguity
- The legal baseline (the AI Act text) establishes staged application dates (most notably 2 Aug 2026 for high‑risk obligations). That is the safest planning baseline because it is the legally adopted text. However, the Commission’s Digital Omnibus proposal (19 Nov 2025) and the reality that harmonised standards and guidance are not fully in place introduce real uncertainty about how, when and at what pace national authorities and the Commission will enforce particular duties. The Omnibus could (if adopted) delay or change the way certain obligations bite in practice; conversely, if it stalls, the original dates remain in force. Because these are active and fast‑moving political and technical processes, product teams should plan for the legal dates but watch the Omnibus, EDPB/EDPS guidance, and standards publication calendars closely. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng))
