Short answer (current as of 15 March 2026)

1) Most recent enforcement milestone
- The most recent milestone that actually came into effect was 2 August 2025 — on that date “Chapter III Section 4, Chapter V, Chapter VII and Chapter XII and Article 78” of the AI Act came into application. That package includes the governance/transparency provisions and the specific obligations aimed at providers of general‑purpose AI models (GPAI). ([ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113?utm_source=openai))

2) Which obligations apply next
- The next big applicability date is 2 August 2026 — this is when the majority of the AI Act’s substantive obligations become applicable across the EU (including many rules for high‑risk AI systems listed in Annex III, broad transparency duties and many deployer/provider obligations). Expect enforcement activity to increase after that date. ([ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113?utm_source=openai))  
- Note separately: Article 6(1) and the corresponding obligations (and certain rules for AI embedded in regulated products) apply from 2 August 2027 (so teams working in regulated sectors like medical devices, vehicles, etc. should plan for the 2027 timeline in addition to 2026). ([ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113?utm_source=openai))

3) Key uncertainties product teams should keep in mind (concrete / actionable)
- Classification ambiguity (who/what is “high‑risk”): many obligations hinge on whether a system is classed as high‑risk (Article 6 routes and Annex lists). Member‑state/Commission guidance and implementing acts (and possible delegated acts) will materially affect which systems are treated as high‑risk in practice. This is a principal source of legal uncertainty for product classification decisions. ([taylorwessing.com](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2024/11/high-risk-ai-systems?utm_source=openai))  
- Timing and content of delegated/implementing acts and harmonised standards: conformity assessment routes, use of notified bodies, and the practical means to demonstrate compliance depend on harmonised standards, common specifications and implementing acts that are still being developed — and these can shift feasibility, cost and timing for compliance. In short: even if a deadline is fixed, the “how” may not be in place when the deadline arrives. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-act-standardisation?utm_source=openai))  
- GPAI “systemic risk” designation and phased enforcement: the Commission/AI Office can designate certain general‑purpose models as presenting systemic risk; the Commission also issued a voluntary Code of Practice for GPAI (10 July 2025) and signatory routes that can reduce administrative burden — but the AI Office’s phased enforcement approach (different effective dates for new versus existing models) creates staggered obligations for model providers. Expect more detail from the AI Office on exactly how the GPAI rules will be applied to specific models. ([ec.europa.eu](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/api/files/document/print/en/ip_25_1787/IP_25_1787_EN.pdf?utm_source=openai))  
- National enforcement and penalty regimes (heterogeneity): Member States must designate competent authorities and set up enforcement/penalty procedures; actual enforcement practices (focus areas, speed, penalty levels) are likely to vary between Member States in the first years. Plan for cross‑jurisdictional differences (compliance-by-design is the safest route). ([commission.europa.eu](https://commission.europa.eu/news/ai-act-enters-force-2024-08-01_en?utm_source=openai))  
- Interaction with other EU rules (GDPR, product safety, sector law) and potential timeline changes: how the AI Act interoperates with GDPR, medical device law, product safety law, and any future “Digital Omnibus” timeline adjustments (or other legislative fixes) creates legal/operational uncertainty for teams building products that touch multiple regimes. The Commission has proposed/considered tailoring timelines to the availability of standards and guidance in some areas. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/navigating-ai-act?utm_source=openai))

Explicit timing ambiguity / caveats (be precise)
- “Applied” vs “fully enforced”: the Act’s text fixes the application dates (Article 113), but practical enforcement (what competent authorities expect you to produce to show compliance) depends on secondary acts, harmonised standards and guidance that may arrive later; for GPAI the Commission deliberately allowed staggered enforceability (different effective dates for new vs existing models). In practice this means a legal deadline (e.g., 2 Aug 2026) can coexist with practical uncertainty about exactly how to demonstrate compliance on day one. ([ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113?utm_source=openai))  
- Potential timeline adjustments: proposals such as the “Digital Omnibus” and ongoing standardisation work could change practical timelines or introduce contingency triggers (e.g., tying applicability to availability of standards). Monitor Commission and standardisation bodies closely. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/navigating-ai-act?utm_source=openai))

Practical recommendations for a product team (brief)
- Treat the Act as already in force and plan for the 2 Aug 2026 applicability milestone for most obligations — but treat August 2025 GPAI obligations as already applicable for GPAI activities. ([ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113?utm_source=openai))  
- Start a classification & inventory exercise now (map models/use cases to Annex III/Article 6 pathways), and run a gap analysis against high‑risk requirements (risk management, data governance, documentation, post‑market monitoring and conformity assessment). ([taylorwessing.com](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2024/11/high-risk-ai-systems?utm_source=openai))  
- Track delegated/implementing acts, harmonised standards and AI Office guidance (and consider adopting the GPAI Code of Practice if you are a model provider — it can be a pragmatic path to demonstrable compliance). ([ec.europa.eu](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/api/files/document/print/en/ip_25_1787/IP_25_1787_EN.pdf?utm_source=openai))
